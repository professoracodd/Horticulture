Introduction

What Are Cover Crops & Green Manures?

Cover crops are any non-cash crop grown in addition to the primary cash crop to keep the soil covered as much of the year as possible. Their benefits include increasing soil organic matter, reducing erosion, improving soil structure, promoting water infiltration, and limiting pest and disease outbreaks.

Green manuring involves mowing, crimping, and/or tilling any field or forage crop while green or soon after flowering. High-biomass legume green manures can return 100–200 lbs/acre of nitrogen to the soil, potentially eliminating synthetic fertilizer needs entirely.

By around 300 BC, the Greeks and Romans were already planting legumes for their soil fertility advantages. Native Americans developed the Three Sisters concept — corn, beans, and squash — for the synergistic benefits of diversity.

Soil Health

Organic Matter, Soil Structure & Microbial Life

Cover crops provide the biomass (carbon) and diversity needed for soil microorganism populations to thrive. Research across 81 studies found that soil total bacteria and total fungi increased 7% to 31% when cover crops were used, compared to systems with no cover crop.

🦠

Microbial Diversity

A greater fungi-to-bacteria ratio is found in cover-cropped systems, indicating a healthier soil food web and improved nutrient cycling capacity.

🧲

Soil Aggregation

Glomalin produced by mycorrhizal fungi binds soil particles into aggregates, improving aeration, water infiltration, and root penetration.

💧

Water Infiltration

Well-aggregated, cover-cropped soils act like a sponge, absorbing even unprecedented rain events while bare soils generate destructive runoff.

🌿

Humus Formation

Increased organic matter influences soil humus — the stable end product of decay — providing a wide range of crop production benefits.

🌡️

Soil Temperature

Cover crop mulch reduces extreme soil temperatures, protecting beneficial microbes and creating a more hospitable environment for root growth.

🌾

Organic Matter Input

A green manure crop contributes organic matter comparable to 9–13 tons/acre of farmyard manure, maintaining or building soil fertility over time.

Nitrogen Production

Growing Your Own Fertility

Leguminous cover crops can accumulate 40 to 200 pounds of nitrogen per acre. About 50% of that nitrogen becomes available to the following crop. The C:N ratio of plant material governs how quickly nutrients become available to subsequent crops.

Best Practices

3 Steps for Optimum Nitrogen Carryover

Follow these practices to maximize nitrogen transfer from legume cover crops to subsequent cash crops.

01

Inoculate the Seed

Apply the correct Rhizobium bacterial inoculant before planting. Match species to inoculant: use R. leguminosarum bv. Viceae for peas and vetch; bv. Trifolii for clovers; and Sinorhizobium meliloti for alfalfa.

02

Terminate at Bud or Early Flower

Nitrogen content in leaves drops rapidly after flowering as it moves into seeds. Terminate at bud stage or early flower for peak nitrogen contribution to your cash crop.

03

Maintain Good Management

Adequate water, proper soil fertility levels, and correct soil pH are all essential for good nodulation and effective nitrogen fixation throughout the growing season.

Cover Crop Systems

Fitting Covers Into Your Rotation

Cover crops can fit into many different cropping systems — between cash crops, interseeded into standing crops, or as full-season sods. Choose your system type to learn more.

Winter Cover Crops

Sown in late summer or autumn, winter cover crops remain in place until spring to provide soil cover during the cold months. Growers often choose legumes for added nitrogen fixation.

In northern states, the cover crop must tolerate hard winters. Good choices include hairy vetch, crimson clover, annual rye, and Austrian winter peas (in more southerly regions of the North). They can be established by aerial seeding into maturing cash crops, or by drilling/broadcasting after harvest.

Summer Cover Crops

Warm-season covers fill a niche in rotations, improve poor soils, or prepare land for perennial crops. Legumes such as cowpeas, soybeans, annual sweet clover, or velvet beans add nitrogen and organic matter.

Non-legumes like sorghum-sudangrass, millet, or buckwheat provide biomass, smother weeds, and improve soil tilth. Buckwheat is a fast-growing option that provides excellent pollinator habitat between summer and fall crops.

Smother Crops

Grown in any season between cash crops, smother crops are used primarily for weed suppression. Weeds flourish on bare soil, but cover crops compete for space and light, shading the soil and reducing weed establishment opportunities.

The soil-loosening effect of deep-rooting covers also reduces weed populations that thrive in compacted soils. Field peas are known for their weed-control benefits due to their high biomass productivity.

Interseeding / Intercropping

Cover crops are planted into a standing cash crop and can remain for varying amounts of time. Interseeded covers provide a living mulch to suppress weeds and reduce soil erosion while enhancing fertility.

Examples include overseeding hairy vetch into corn at the last cultivation, drilling sweet clover into small grains, or broadcasting annual ryegrass into vegetables. This is particularly useful on smaller farms where all land is in production but a cover crop still needs to get established.

Catch Crops

Established after harvesting the main crop, catch crops primarily reduce nutrient leaching. For example, planting cereal rye following corn harvest scavenges residual nitrogen, reducing groundwater contamination risk.

Annual ryegrass (as opposed to cereal rye) is particularly good at scavenging phosphorus. These double-duty crops function as catch crops and winter covers simultaneously, maximizing their benefit to the farm system.

Forage Crops in Rotation

Forage crops function as cover crops when occupying land for pasturage or haying, and as green manures when eventually incorporated or terminated for no-till planting. Research shows grassland soils naturally accumulate organic matter through extensive root systems.

A grass-clover ley period equivalent to 30–40% of the crop rotation sequence (3–4 years in a 10-year rotation) is needed to meaningfully increase aggregate stability and soil carbon concentration.

Termination Methods

Terminating Cover Crops for No-Till Mulch

Choosing the right termination method depends on your scale, organic certification status, timing, and target cash crop. Each approach has tradeoffs in cost, effectiveness, and ecological impact.

🌀 Roller-Crimping

A drum with V-shaped chevron blades rolls over the cover crop, laying it down and crimping stems to interrupt sap flow. Can be combined with planting in a single tractor pass. Best done in hot, dry weather at 50% bloom for legumes.

Organic-approved Large & small scale

🚜 Mowing / Flail Mowing

Effective for rye when mowed at pollination stage (anthers fully extended). Flail mowers create more uniformly distributed mulch than rotary cutters. Sickle-bar mowers also work but unchopped stalks are harder to plant into.

Organic-approved All scales

🌿 Tilling / Incorporation

Cover crops can be tilled into the top 2 inches for aerobic decomposition (per Nordell method) or deeper. Promotes faster nutrient release but releases CO₂ and eliminates weed suppression benefits of surface mulch.

Fast nutrient release Market gardens

❄️ Winter Kill

Plant cold-sensitive species (spring oats, buckwheat, sorghum, winter peas in northern regions) that naturally die at freezing temperatures. Provides a dead mulch through winter. Ideal for early spring planting situations.

No equipment needed Low-rainfall regions

🌾 Undercutting

A cultivator fitted with sweeps cuts roots just below the soil surface, then a furrower covers them over. Favored by the Nordells, often followed by a spring-tooth harrow. Minimizes disturbance while effectively terminating growth.

Minimal disturbance Organic-approved

🧪 Herbicide

The most commonly used tool in conventional no-till systems. However, research shows herbicides can disrupt earthworms, inhibit nitrogen fixation and nitrification, and cause site-specific increases in disease. Not allowed in organic production.

Conventional only Fastest & lowest cost
Advanced Technique

Planting Green

Planting green means establishing summer cash crops directly into the standing cover crop before terminating it. Soils under a living cover tend to be slightly drier — a real benefit in wet springs.

This approach allows the cover to accumulate more biomass before termination. Annual rye doesn't grow much in winter but takes off strongly in spring — waiting to terminate maximizes biomass for roller-crimping.

Rick Clark's System: On 1,200 certified-organic acres in Indiana, Clark sows corn and soybeans into live-standing rye and clover, then crimps the cover crop 3–30 days after planting. Result: no starter fertilizer, no fungicide, no insecticide, no phosphorus/potassium/nitrogen fertilizer, and 48% less diesel use.

Species Guide

Common Cover Crop Species

Select the right species or mix for your climate, rotation, and goals. Each category offers unique benefits to your farming system.

Grasses provide high biomass and carbon for soil organic matter. They pair excellently with legumes — the carbon of grass slows nitrogen release from legumes, better timing nutrient availability for cash crops. Cereal rye is the most winter-hardy and widely adapted.

Cereal rye Triticale Wheat Barley Millet Oat Annual ryegrass Sorghum-sudangrass

Broadleaf cover crops offer diversity in the soil microbiome and can break pest and disease cycles. Buckwheat is a fast-growing favorite that produces abundant flowers attractive to beneficial insects and pollinators. Phacelia is also a top pollinator plant.

Flax Sunflower Buckwheat Safflower Phacelia Chicory

Brassicas are known for their deep-rooting ability (especially radish), which breaks up subsoil compaction. They decompose quickly in spring, releasing nutrients for early crops. Some brassicas have biofumigation properties that help suppress soilborne pathogens and nematodes.

Radish Turnip Kale Canola Mustard Camelina

Case Study

Growing Fertility at Three Springs Farm

Emily Oakley & Michael Appel · Eastern Oklahoma · Certified Organic

90% of Fertility Grown On-Farm

Emily Oakley and Michael Appel cultivate more than 40 different crops and 150 varieties on their small diversified vegetable farm. Their goal: demonstrate the economic viability of small-scale farming while minimizing external fertility inputs.

The farm is laid out with three acres of vegetables and three acres in cover at all times. Of four fields, two are in cash crops and two are in fallow cover all year. A common rotation starts with a spring cash crop → summer cover → fall/winter cover → full year in fallow covers → repeat with a different spring crop.

Economics

The Economics of Cover Crops

Cover crops involve real costs — seed, equipment, management time — but their benefits in reduced inputs and improved yields often exceed those costs, especially over time as soil health builds.

Conservation

Soil & Water Conservation

The soil conservation benefits of cover crops extend beyond protecting bare soil during non-crop periods. Cover crop mulch increases water infiltration, reduces surface evaporation, and prevents soil crusting — making farms more resilient to both drought and flood.

California Floods, January 2023: Central California received over 10 inches of rain in several weeks. Almond orchards with cover crops had little or no standing water after the event — soils acted as a sponge. Neighboring uncovered orchards couldn't infiltrate the water and suffered severe flooding. At the time, only 4% of California farmland acres were in cover crops.
